Prehistoric Era Overview

The Prehistoric Era covers human history before written records. This vast period saw the development of early human societies, tools, and cultural practices that laid the foundation for civilization.

Key characteristics:

  • Development of stone tools and technologies
  • Transition from nomadic to settled lifestyles
  • Emergence of art and symbolic thought
  • Development of agriculture and animal domestication
  • Creation of first permanent settlements

Paleolithic Age (Old Stone Age)

Also known as the Old Stone Age (2.5 million - 10,000 BCE):

  • Development of simple stone tools (Oldowan and Acheulean tools)
  • Control of fire (c. 1 million BCE)
  • Hunter-gatherer societies with nomadic lifestyles
  • Emergence of art (cave paintings like Lascaux, c. 17,000 BCE)
  • Development of language and symbolic thought
  • Use of animal skins for clothing
  • Burial practices suggesting spiritual beliefs

Mesolithic Age (Middle Stone Age)

The transitional period (10,000 - 8,000 BCE):

  • Smaller, more refined stone tools (microliths)
  • Early domestication of dogs
  • Increased use of bone and antler tools
  • Development of more complex social structures
  • Beginning of semi-permanent settlements
  • Improved fishing techniques
  • Evidence of early trade networks

Neolithic Age (New Stone Age)

The New Stone Age (8,000 - 3,000 BCE) brought revolutionary changes:

  • Agricultural Revolution (fertile crescent c. 10,000 BCE)
  • Domestication of plants (wheat, barley, rice) and animals (sheep, goats, cattle)
  • Permanent settlements (Jericho c. 9,000 BCE, Çatalhöyük c. 7,500 BCE)
  • Pottery development (Jōmon pottery c. 14,000 BCE)
  • Megalithic structures (Stonehenge began c. 3,000 BCE)
  • Weaving of textiles
  • Increased population density

Development of Agriculture

The Neolithic Revolution marked humanity's transition from hunting-gathering to agriculture:

  • Fertile Crescent (wheat/barley): 10,000-8,000 BCE
  • Yangtze/Yellow River (rice/millet): 7,000 BCE
  • Mesoamerica (maize/beans): 5,000 BCE
  • Andes (potatoes): 4,000 BCE
  • Led to population growth, food surplus, and specialization
  • Development of irrigation systems
  • Creation of calendar systems to track planting seasons

First Human Settlements

Early permanent villages emerged worldwide:

  • Jericho (Jordan Valley): Walls built c. 8,000 BCE
  • Çatalhöyük (Turkey): Large Neolithic town c. 7,500 BCE
  • Mehrgarh (Pakistan): Early farming community c. 7,000 BCE
  • Banpo (China): Yangshao culture village c. 5,000 BCE
  • Tell es-Sultan (Jordan): Continuously inhabited for millennia
  • Byblos (Lebanon): One of oldest continuously inhabited cities
  • Eridu (Iraq): Considered first city by Sumerians

Bronze Age Overview

The Bronze Age (c. 3300–1200 BCE) saw the rise of the first complex civilizations, characterized by urban development, early writing systems, and bronze metallurgy.

Key developments:

  • Emergence of first cities and states
  • Invention of writing systems
  • Development of bronze tools and weapons
  • Construction of monumental architecture
  • Establishment of trade networks

Sumerians in Mesopotamia

The world's first civilization emerged in Sumer (southern Mesopotamia):

  • City-states: Ur, Uruk, Lagash, Eridu (from c. 2900 BCE)
  • Development of wheel (c. 3500 BCE) and plow
  • Epic of Gilgamesh - earliest known literature
  • Advanced mathematics (base-60 system)
  • First law codes (Ur-Nammu code c. 2100 BCE)
  • Complex irrigation systems
  • Lunar calendar

Invention of Writing (Cuneiform)

Cuneiform emerged in Sumer around 3400-3300 BCE:

  • Evolved from pictographs to wedge-shaped signs
  • Initially used for accounting and administrative records
  • Written on clay tablets with reed styluses
  • Used for multiple languages: Sumerian, Akkadian, Hittite
  • Deciphered in the 19th century by Henry Rawlinson
  • Enabled recording of laws, literature, and history
  • Paved the way for later writing systems

Ziggurats and City-States

Ziggurats were massive stepped pyramids that dominated Mesopotamian cities:

  • Religious centers dedicated to city deities
  • Built with sun-baked bricks and faced with fired bricks
  • Examples: Great Ziggurat of Ur (c. 2100 BCE), Etemenanki in Babylon
  • City-states were independent political units with:
  • - Urban center surrounded by agricultural land
  • - Own government, laws, and patron deity
  • - Defensive walls for protection
  • Competition between city-states led to early warfare

Ancient Egypt (Old Kingdom to New Kingdom)

Egyptian civilization developed along the Nile River:

  • Old Kingdom (2686-2181 BCE): Age of pyramids
  • Middle Kingdom (2055-1650 BCE): Reunification and expansion
  • New Kingdom (1550-1069 BCE): Imperial Egypt, Valley of Kings
  • Hieroglyphic writing developed c. 3200 BCE
  • Complex religious beliefs with many deities
  • Advanced medicine, mathematics, and astronomy
  • Pharaohs considered divine rulers

Pyramids of Giza

The Giza pyramid complex includes:

  • Great Pyramid of Khufu (c. 2560 BCE): Tallest ancient structure
  • Pyramid of Khafre: With intact limestone casing at top
  • Pyramid of Menkaure: Smallest of the three
  • Great Sphinx: Monumental sculpture with lion's body and human head
  • Constructed with precise alignment to cardinal points
  • Built by skilled workers, not slaves as previously thought
  • Represented the pharaoh's journey to the afterlife

Iron Age Overview

The Iron Age (c. 1200-500 BCE) was characterized by:

  • Widespread use of iron tools and weapons
  • Collapse of Bronze Age civilizations
  • Rise of new empires and kingdoms
  • Development of alphabetic writing systems
  • Expansion of trade networks
  • Emergence of monotheistic religions

Mycenaean Civilization (Greece)

Late Bronze Age Greek civilization (c. 1600-1100 BCE):

  • Centered around fortified palaces
  • Linear B script - earliest form of Greek
  • Known for warrior culture and epic poetry
  • Major sites: Mycenae, Tiryns, Pylos
  • Famous for "Mask of Agamemnon" gold death mask
  • Megaron architecture: central hall with hearth
  • Declined during the Bronze Age Collapse c. 1200 BCE

Assyrian Empire

The Neo-Assyrian Empire (911-609 BCE) was a military powerhouse:

  • Developed first large professional army
  • Used iron weapons, siege engines, and cavalry
  • Capital at Nineveh with library containing 30,000 tablets
  • Known for brutal warfare and mass deportations
  • Extensive road system for rapid troop movement
  • Rulers: Ashurnasirpal II, Sargon II, Ashurbanipal
  • Conquered Egypt, Babylon, and most of Near East

Military Innovations

Iron Age military advancements:

  • Widespread use of iron weapons (stronger, cheaper than bronze)
  • Development of cavalry units
  • Siege warfare technology: battering rams, siege towers
  • Assyrian innovations: Pioneered combined arms tactics
  • Chariots evolved into heavier, more specialized forms
  • Standardized equipment for large armies
  • Early forms of military engineering

Hebrews / Israelites

Ancient Semitic people of the Levant:

  • Established kingdoms of Israel and Judah
  • Developed monotheistic worship of Yahweh
  • Biblical accounts describe early history and kings
  • King David (c. 1000 BCE) established Jerusalem as capital
  • King Solomon built First Temple
  • Assyrian conquest of Israel (722 BCE)
  • Babylonian captivity (586 BCE) and later return

Monotheism and the Torah

Revolutionary religious development:

  • Hebrews developed exclusive worship of one God (Yahweh)
  • Torah (first five books of Hebrew Bible) compiled
  • Concept of covenant between God and his people
  • Prophets emphasized ethical monotheism
  • Influenced later Abrahamic religions
  • Developed during Babylonian exile (6th century BCE)
  • Synagogue worship emerged during exile

Classical Civilizations Overview

Period of great cultural achievements and empires:

  • Development of philosophy, democracy, and science
  • Expansion of trade routes like the Silk Road
  • Rise and fall of large empires
  • Spread of universal religions
  • Advances in art, architecture, and literature

Ancient Greece

Foundations of Western civilization (c. 800-146 BCE):

  • Birthplace of democracy, philosophy, and Olympic Games
  • Homeric epics: Iliad and Odyssey
  • Development of classical architecture orders
  • Thriving city-states with distinct identities
  • Persian Wars (499-449 BCE) and Peloponnesian War (431-404 BCE)
  • Legacy in art, literature, and political thought

City-States: Athens and Sparta

Contrasting Greek poleis:

  • Athens: Birthplace of democracy, cultural center
  • - Direct democracy with citizen participation
  • - Naval power with Delian League
  • - Golden Age under Pericles (461-429 BCE)
  • Sparta: Militaristic oligarchy
  • - Agoge education system for warriors
  • - Helot serf class worked the land
  • - Peloponnesian League dominance

Democracy and Philosophy

Athenian contributions to governance and thought:

  • Cleisthenes established democracy (508 BCE)
  • Citizens participated in Assembly and juries
  • Socrates (469-399 BCE): Socratic method
  • Plato (428-348 BCE): Theory of Forms, Academy
  • Aristotle (384-322 BCE): Logic, scientific method
  • Sophists: Professional teachers of rhetoric
  • Philosophical schools: Stoicism, Epicureanism

Alexander the Great & Hellenism

Macedonian conquest and its cultural impact:

  • Alexander III of Macedon (356-323 BCE)
  • Conquered Persian Empire by age 25
  • Reached India before troops mutinied
  • Founded cities including Alexandria in Egypt
  • Hellenistic culture blended Greek and Eastern elements
  • Spread Greek language, art, and ideas
  • Divided into successor kingdoms after his death

Roman Republic and Empire

Rome's evolution from city-state to empire:

  • Republic (509-27 BCE): Senate and elected magistrates
  • Punic Wars (264-146 BCE) against Carthage
  • Imperial period (27 BCE-476 CE) began with Augustus
  • Pax Romana (27 BCE-180 CE): Era of relative peace
  • Engineering marvels: Roads, aqueducts, Colosseum
  • Legal system influenced modern law
  • Spread of Christianity in later empire

Late Antiquity Overview

Transition period between classical antiquity and Middle Ages:

  • Decline of Western Roman Empire
  • Rise of Christianity and institutional church
  • Spread of monasticism
  • Emergence of Byzantine Empire in the East
  • Development of new cultural syntheses
  • Barbarian kingdoms established in West

Fall of the Western Roman Empire

Complex process spanning centuries:

  • Economic troubles: Inflation, taxation issues
  • Military challenges: Mercenary armies, border pressures
  • Political instability: Frequent emperor changes
  • Barbarian invasions: Visigoths, Vandals, Huns
  • Sack of Rome: 410 CE by Visigoths, 455 CE by Vandals
  • Last emperor Romulus Augustulus deposed 476 CE
  • Eastern Empire continued as Byzantine Empire

Byzantine Empire

Eastern continuation of the Roman Empire:

  • Capital Constantinople (modern Istanbul)
  • Lasted nearly 1000 years after Western collapse
  • Justinian I (527-565 CE): Corpus Juris Civilis, Hagia Sophia
  • Greek language and Orthodox Christianity
  • Iconoclasm controversy (8th-9th centuries)
  • Preserved classical Greek knowledge
  • Fell to Ottoman Turks in 1453

Christianization of Europe

Spread of Christianity across the continent:

  • Constantine's Edict of Milan (313 CE) legalized Christianity
  • Theodosius made Christianity state religion (380 CE)
  • Monasticism spread: St. Benedict, St. Patrick
  • Conversion of barbarian tribes: Franks, Goths
  • Missionaries: St. Augustine to England, Cyril and Methodius to Slavs
  • Development of papal authority
  • Christianity became unifying cultural force

Spread of Buddhism

Expansion beyond India during ancient period:

  • Ashoka the Great (3rd century BCE) sent missionaries
  • Spread along Silk Road to Central Asia
  • Reached China during Han Dynasty (1st century CE)
  • Transmission to Southeast Asia via maritime routes
  • Development of Mahayana Buddhism
  • Monasteries became centers of learning and culture
  • Syncretism with local traditions

Migrations & Invasions

Mass movements of peoples in late antiquity:

  • Huns pushed Germanic tribes into Roman territory
  • Visigoths sacked Rome 410 CE, settled in Spain
  • Vandals crossed to North Africa, sacked Rome 455 CE
  • Angles, Saxons, and Jutes migrated to Britain
  • Franks established kingdom in Gaul (France)
  • Ostrogoths ruled Italy until Byzantine reconquest
  • Lombards invaded Italy after Byzantine withdrawal
