British Colonial Era Overview (1757–1947)

The period of British rule in India transformed the subcontinent politically, economically, and socially. What began as a trading relationship evolved into full colonial domination.

Key characteristics:

  • Establishment of the East India Company's political power
  • Economic exploitation through land revenue systems
  • Introduction of Western education and administrative systems
  • Development of infrastructure like railways and telegraph
  • Social reforms and emergence of Indian nationalism
  • Gradual transfer of power to the British Crown

Battle of Plassey (1757)

This battle marked the beginning of British political control in India:

  • British East India Company vs. Nawab of Bengal Siraj-ud-Daulah
  • Robert Clive secured victory through betrayal and conspiracy
  • Mir Jafar installed as puppet ruler of Bengal
  • Company gained control of Bengal's treasury
  • Established British military supremacy in India
  • Paved the way for further territorial expansion

Battle of Buxar (1764)

A decisive battle that confirmed British dominance in Bengal:

  • British forces led by Hector Munro
  • Defeated combined forces of Mir Qasim, Shuja-ud-Daulah (Nawab of Awadh), and Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II
  • Resulted in the Treaty of Allahabad (1765)
  • Company gained Diwani rights (revenue collection) for Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa
  • Established British as the dominant power in eastern India

Regulating Act (1773)

The first step by British Parliament to regulate East India Company affairs:

  • Created the office of Governor-General of Bengal
  • Warren Hastings became first Governor-General
  • Established a Supreme Court at Calcutta
  • Prohibited company servants from private trade
  • Required company to report on revenue matters to Parliament

Permanent Settlement (1793)

Land revenue system introduced by Lord Cornwallis in Bengal:

  • Zamindars recognized as landowners
  • Fixed revenue demand permanently
  • Created a class of loyal landlords
  • Led to exploitation of peasants
  • Stagnated agricultural development

Death of Tipu Sultan (1799)

The fall of Mysore's "Tiger" ended major resistance in South India:

  • Killed during the Fourth Anglo-Mysore War
  • Siege of Seringapatam by British forces
  • Known for innovative administration and military tactics
  • Early adopter of rocket artillery
  • Developed alliances with French against British

Defeat of Marathas (1818)

End of the Third Anglo-Maratha War established British supremacy:

  • Peshwa Baji Rao II surrendered
  • Maratha Confederacy dissolved
  • British gained control of most of western and central India
  • Established the Bombay Presidency
  • Paved way for complete British dominance

Abolition of Sati (1829)

Landmark social reform by Governor-General Lord William Bentinck:

  • Sati practice involved widow immolation on husband's pyre
  • Ban enforced in Bengal Presidency
  • Supported by reformers like Raja Ram Mohan Roy
  • Faced opposition from orthodox groups
  • Marked beginning of social reform era

First Railway (1853)

India's first passenger train revolutionized transportation:

  • Ran between Bombay (Mumbai) and Thane
  • Covered 34 km in about 45 minutes
  • Built by Great Indian Peninsula Railway
  • Accelerated economic integration
  • Facilitated movement of troops and goods

First War of Independence (1857)

Major uprising against British rule:

  • Began as sepoy mutiny in Meerut
  • Spread to Delhi, Lucknow, Kanpur, Jhansi
  • Key leaders: Rani Lakshmibai, Nana Saheb, Bahadur Shah Zafar
  • Triggered by greased cartridges controversy
  • Resulted in end of East India Company rule

Beginning of Crown Rule (1858)

Transfer of power from East India Company to British Crown:

  • Government of India Act 1858
  • Created office of Secretary of State for India
  • Governor-General became Viceroy (Lord Canning first)
  • Established Indian Civil Service
  • Direct rule by British Parliament began

Formation of Indian National Congress (1885)

The INC became the leading voice of Indian nationalism:

  • Founded by A.O. Hume with W.C. Bonnerjee as first president
  • Initial moderate phase sought constitutional reforms
  • Provided platform for educated Indians
  • Annual sessions across India
  • Later led by Tilak, Gandhi, Nehru

Partition of Bengal (1905)

Controversial decision by Lord Curzon:

  • Divided Bengal into Hindu-majority west and Muslim-majority east
  • Officially for administrative efficiency
  • Widely seen as "divide and rule" policy
  • Triggered Swadeshi Movement and boycott of British goods
  • Reversed in 1911 due to nationalist pressure

Muslim League Founded (1906)

Establishment of political organization for Muslim interests:

  • Founded in Dhaka by Nawab Salimullah Khan
  • Aga Khan III served as first president
  • Initially advocated for separate electorates
  • Later championed the Pakistan movement
  • Key leaders included Muhammad Ali Jinnah

Capital Shift to Delhi (1911)

Transfer of British India's capital from Calcutta to Delhi:

  • Announced at Delhi Durbar by King George V
  • Partially to counter Bengali nationalism
  • New Delhi designed by Edwin Lutyens and Herbert Baker
  • Construction completed in 1931
  • Positioned government closer to geographical center

Nationalist Era Overview (1919–1947)

This period saw the growth of mass movements against British rule:

  • Shift from moderate demands to calls for complete independence
  • Emergence of Gandhi and non-violent civil disobedience
  • Growth of revolutionary movements
  • Rise of communalism and demand for partition
  • World War II accelerated the independence process
  • Final transfer of power and partition in 1947

Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (1919)

Turning point in India's freedom struggle:

  • British troops under Gen. Dyer fired on peaceful gathering
  • Occurred in Amritsar during Baisakhi festival
  • Official figures: 379 killed, 1,200 wounded
  • Triggered nationwide outrage and protests
  • Tagore renounced his knighthood in protest

Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–1922)

First nationwide mass movement led by Gandhi:

  • Launched in response to Rowlatt Act and Jallianwala Bagh
  • Called for boycott of British institutions
  • Surrender of titles and honors
  • Withdrawal from government schools and courts
  • Suspended after Chauri Chaura violence (1922)

Simon Commission (1928)

All-British committee to review constitutional reforms:

  • No Indian member in the commission
  • Met with nationwide protests and black flags
  • "Simon Go Back" became popular slogan
  • Lala Lajpat Rai died from injuries during protest
  • Led to Nehru Report demanding dominion status

Salt March (Dandi March) (1930)

Landmark act of civil disobedience against salt tax:

  • Gandhi marched 385 km from Sabarmati to Dandi
  • Lasted 24 days with growing participation
  • Produced salt by evaporating seawater
  • Inspired nationwide civil disobedience
  • Over 60,000 Indians imprisoned

Gandhi-Irwin Pact (1931)

Truce agreement between Gandhi and Viceroy Irwin:

  • Congress agreed to suspend civil disobedience
  • Government agreed to release political prisoners
  • Allowed salt production for coastal communities
  • Congress participated in Second Round Table Conference
  • Temporary truce in independence struggle

Government of India Act (1935)

Most significant constitutional reform before independence:

  • Provided provincial autonomy
  • Established dyarchy at the center
  • Created Federal Court of India
  • Separate electorates expanded
  • Basis for independent India's constitution

Quit India Movement (1942)

Massive civil disobedience movement during WWII:

  • Gandhi's call: "Do or Die"
  • Demanded immediate British withdrawal
  • Entire Congress leadership arrested
  • Widespread protests and underground activities
  • Violently suppressed by British authorities

Royal Indian Navy Mutiny (1946)

Large-scale revolt by Indian sailors:

  • Began on HMIS Talwar in Bombay
  • Spread to 78 ships and shore establishments
  • Over 20,000 sailors participated
  • Raised nationalist slogans and flags
  • Convinced British that Indian armed forces couldn't be relied upon

Indian Independence (1947)

Transfer of power and partition:

  • Indian Independence Act passed by British Parliament
  • Two independent dominions: India and Pakistan
  • Lord Mountbatten last Viceroy and first Governor-General
  • Jawaharlal Nehru became India's first Prime Minister
  • Mass migration and violence during partition

Post-Independence India Overview (1947–2025)

India's journey as an independent nation:

  • Integration of princely states into Indian Union
  • Adoption of democratic constitution
  • Mixed economy model with Five-Year Plans
  • Green Revolution and economic liberalization
  • Emergence as global IT power and nuclear state
  • Challenges of poverty, communalism, and regional conflicts

Nehruvian Era (1947–1964)

Jawaharlal Nehru's tenure as first Prime Minister:

  • Integration of princely states by Sardar Patel
  • Assassination of Mahatma Gandhi (1948)
  • Constitution adopted (1950) making India a republic
  • First Five-Year Plan (1951-56) focused on agriculture
  • First general elections (1952) - world's largest democracy
  • States Reorganization Act (1956) on linguistic basis
  • Sino-Indian War (1962)

Indira Gandhi Era (1964–1984)

Period dominated by India's first female Prime Minister:

  • Second Indo-Pak War (1965)
  • Green Revolution transformed agriculture
  • Bank nationalization (1969)
  • Third Indo-Pak War (1971) and liberation of Bangladesh
  • Pokhran-I nuclear test (1974)
  • Emergency imposed (1975-77)
  • Operation Blue Star (1984)
  • Assassination of Indira Gandhi (1984)
  • Anti-Sikh riots (1984)

Rajiv Gandhi & Liberalization (1984–1999)

Transition to modern economy and politics:

  • Rajiv Gandhi became youngest PM (1984)
  • Computerization and telecom revolution
  • Bofors scandal (1989)
  • Assassination of Rajiv Gandhi (1991)
  • Economic liberalization by PM Rao and FM Singh (1991)
  • Babri Masjid demolition (1992)
  • Pokhran-II nuclear tests (1998)
  • Kargil War (1999)

21st Century India (2000–2025)

India's emergence as a global power:

  • UPA Era (2004–2014)
    • Manmohan Singh as PM
    • Right to Information Act (2005)
    • Mumbai 26/11 attacks (2008)
    • Commonwealth Games (2010)
    • Nirbhaya case and criminal law reforms (2013)
  • NDA Era (2014–Present)
    • Narendra Modi becomes PM (2014)
    • Demonetization (2016)
    • Goods and Services Tax (GST) introduced (2017)
    • Balakot Air Strikes (2019)
    • Abrogation of Article 370 (2019)
    • Citizenship Amendment Act (CAA) passed (2019)
    • COVID-19 pandemic (2020-21)
    • Farmers' protests (2021)
    • G20 Summit hosted (2023)
    • General Elections (2024)

Science & Technology

India's advancements in the 21st century:

  • ISRO Milestones
    • Chandrayaan-1 (2008): Confirmed water on moon
    • Mars Orbiter Mission (2013): First Asian nation to reach Mars orbit
    • Chandrayaan-2 (2019): Orbiter still operational
    • Chandrayaan-3 (2023): First to land near lunar south pole
    • Aditya-L1 (2023): India's first solar mission
  • Digital India initiative
  • Aadhaar: World's largest biometric ID system
  • Startup India: Creation of unicorn startups
  • Make in India and Atmanirbhar Bharat initiatives
  • Semiconductor manufacturing push

Diplomacy

India's evolving global position:

  • Strengthening of Quad alliance (US, Japan, Australia)
  • Leadership of Global South
  • Advocacy for UN Security Council permanent seat
  • Neighborhood First policy
  • Act East policy
  • Strategic autonomy in foreign policy
  • G20 presidency (2023)
  • Expansion of BRICS membership

Culture & Sports

India's soft power achievements:

  • Global Events Hosted
    • Commonwealth Games (2010)
    • ICC Cricket World Cups (2011, 2023)
    • G20 Summit (2023)
    • FIFA U-17 World Cup (2017)
  • Sports Achievements
    • Neeraj Chopra: Olympic gold in javelin (2020)
    • PV Sindhu: Olympic medals in badminton
    • Indian Premier League: World's premier cricket league
    • Improved performance in Paralympics
  • Global recognition of Indian cinema (Oscar wins)
  • International success of Indian music artists
  • Yoga recognized by UNESCO as cultural heritage
